2.9 Sequences and series

A sequence is an ordered list of objects (usually numbers): x1,x2,…,xnx_1, x_2, \ldots, x_nx1​,x2​,…,xn​. A series is a special type of sequence that arises from the cumulative sum of another sequence: x1+x2+⋯+xnx_1 + x_2 + \cdots + x_nx1​+x2​+⋯+xn​, denoted ∑i=1nxi\sum_{i=1}^n x_i∑i=1n​xi​. The limiting behavior of sequences and series (what happens to them as n→∞n \to \inftyn→∞) is very important to statistics as we are often concerned with what happens to an estimate xnx_nxn​ as we collect more data (nnn here representing the number of observations). In particular, does xnx_nxn​ get closer and closer to the quantity we are trying to estimate?

The study of infinite sequences and series is a big topic; this review is not exhaustive but covers the core definitions and commonly arising results that come up often in statistics.

Infinite sequences

The limit of a sequence is very similar to the limit of a function that we have previously encountered. A sequence ana_nan​ has limit aaa if ana_nan​ gets closer and closer to aaa (i.e., the difference ∣an−a∣\lvert a_n-a\rvert∣an​−a∣ gets smaller and smaller) as nnn goes to infinity. This is denoted an→aa_n \to aan​→a5.

As with functions, an→∞\mathbf{a}_n \to \inftyan​→∞ means that ana_nan​ just keeps getting bigger, with no bound.

If the limit exists, we say that the sequence converges (or is convergent). Otherwise, we say the sequence diverges (or is divergent). If an→∞\mathbf{a}_n \to \inftyan​→∞, people often say that the “limit is infinity”, although keep in mind that if this happens, the sequence diverges (it just diverges in a particular way).

All of the limit laws we discussed earlier for functions are equally valid when stated in terms of sequences. For example, if an→aa_n \to aan​→a and bn→bb_n \to bbn​→b, we have

lim⁡n→∞{anbn}=ab,\lim_{n \to \infty} \{ a_n b_n \} = ab,n→∞lim​{an​bn​}=ab,

just like we did for functions.

Monotone convergence theorem: Another way of establishing that a sequence converges is the monotone convergence theorem, which states that every bounded, monotonic sequence converges.
