2.7 Improper integrals

In statistics, it is very common to encounter integrals that look like this:

∫0∞f(x) dx.\int_0^\infty f(x) \,dx.∫0∞​f(x)dx.

This expression is a little confusing because if the integration region is infinite, then our earlier definition of the integral no longer works. What the expression means is that we’re taking the limit of the definite integrals (all of which are well-defined) as the region gets larger and larger:

∫0∞f(x) dx=lim⁡b→∞∫abf(x) dx.\int_0^\infty f(x) \,dx= \lim_{b \to \infty} \int_a^b f(x) \,dx.∫0∞​f(x)dx=b→∞lim​∫ab​f(x)dx.

For example,

∫0be−x dx=1−e−b;\int_0^b e^{-x} \,dx= 1 - e^{-b};∫0b​e−xdx=1−e−b;

(if you don’t follow this derivation, see here and here). Since lim⁡b→∞e−b=0\lim_{b \to \infty} e^{-b} = 0limb→∞​e−b=0 (see here), we have

∫0∞e−x dx=1.\int_0^\infty e^{-x} \,dx= 1.∫0∞​e−xdx=1.

Integrals with an infinite bound (either upper or lower) are known as improper integrals. There are two other kinds of improper integrals.

Both bounds are infinite: One might expect that ∫−∞∞f(x) dx\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x) \,dx∫−∞∞​f(x)dx would be defined as the limit of ∫−aaf(x) dx\int_{-a}^a f(x) \,dx∫−aa​f(x)dx as a→∞a \to \inftya→∞. But you’d be wrong! The actual definition is:

∫−∞∞f(x) dx=∫−∞0f(x) dx+∫0∞f(x) dx,\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x) \,dx= \int_{-\infty}^0 f(x) \,dx+ \int_0^\infty f(x) \,dx,∫−∞∞​f(x)dx=∫−∞0​f(x)dx+∫0∞​f(x)dx,

provided that both improper integrals exist. To see why, suppose we wanted to calculate ∫−∞∞x dx\int_{-\infty}^\infty x \,dx∫−∞∞​xdx. This integral does not exist, even though lim⁡a→∞∫−aax dx=0\lim_{a \to \infty} \int_{-a}^a x \,dx= 0lima→∞​∫−aa​xdx=0. The problem with saying that ∫−∞∞x dx\int_{-\infty}^\infty x \,dx∫−∞∞​xdx equals 0 is that it depends entirely on how fast the upper and lower bounds are going to infinity. For example, lim⁡a→∞∫−a2ax dx=∞\lim_{a \to \infty} \int_{-a}^{2a} x \,dx= \inftylima→∞​∫−a2a​xdx=∞. Without more information on exactly how fast the upper and lower bounds are going to infinity, ∫−∞∞x dx\int_{-\infty}^\infty x \,dx∫−∞∞​xdx could equal anything.
